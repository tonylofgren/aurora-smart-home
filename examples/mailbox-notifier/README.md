# Mailbox Notifier Example

> *Generated by [aurora@aurora-smart-home (esphome skill)](https://github.com/tonylofgren/aurora-smart-home)*

A battery-powered ESP32 sensor that tells Home Assistant the moment the mailbox lid opens, then immediately goes back to sleep. Target battery life: 6+ months on a single 18650 cell, because the device only wakes on the physical event.

## What this is

A "you have mail" notifier you can build in an afternoon. A reed switch on the mailbox lid acts as the wake-up pin for the ESP32 in deep sleep. When the postal carrier opens the lid, the magnet moves away from the reed switch, the GPIO transitions, the ESP32 wakes up, connects to WiFi, fires a notification through the Home Assistant API, reports battery voltage, and goes straight back to deep sleep.

No timer wake-ups. No polling. The only thing that drains the battery during sleep is RTC current (~10 microamps on a healthy ESP32-WROOM-32) plus a tiny amount through the voltage divider.

## Features

- **Event-driven wake**: Only wakes when the lid moves, never on a timer
- **Mail notification**: Push notification to your phone within seconds
- **Battery voltage monitoring**: With low and critical warnings
- **WiFi signal reporting**: Diagnose mailbox reception remotely
- **Long-silence check**: Warns if no mail in 14 days (device may have died)
- **Static IP + fast_connect**: Cuts the wake-to-notify time roughly in half

## Hardware list

### Core

| Component | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| ESP32-WROOM-32 dev board | Older but proven. Solid RTC GPIO behaviour. ESP32-S3 also works. |
| Reed switch (normally open) | Glass tube type, with a small neodymium magnet |
| 18650 LiPo cell, 3000 mAh+ | Chosen for cold-tolerance down to about -20 C |
| TP4056 + protection module | USB-C variant with low-voltage cutoff at 3.0V |
| MT3608 boost or direct 3.7V to 3.3V LDO | LDO is simpler and quieter for sleep currents |
| 2x 100k ohm resistors | Voltage divider for battery monitoring |
| IP65 or IP66 plastic enclosure | Small, with a cable gland |

### Recommended extras

- **External 2.4 GHz antenna (uFL pigtail to SMA)**: see WiFi section below
- **Silica gel desiccant packets**: 2 to 4 grams, replaceable every 6 months
- **Self-amalgamating tape**: seals cable entries permanently
- **Conformal coating** (acrylic, brushable): protects the PCB from condensation

## Wiring

```
ESP32-WROOM-32
|
+-- GPIO33 ----[reed switch]----GND
|              (mounted on mailbox lid frame,
|               magnet glued to the lid itself)
|
+-- GPIO34 ----+---- 100k ----+---- BAT+
|              |              |
|              +---- 100k ----+---- GND
|              (voltage divider, midpoint to GPIO34)
|
+-- 3V3 -------- LDO output
+-- GND -------- Common ground, battery negative,
                 protection module B-, reed switch other leg
```

GPIO33 is RTC-capable on ESP32-WROOM-32, which is what makes the wake-from-deep-sleep trick work. Do not move the reed switch to a non-RTC pin or wake stops working.

## Installation

1. **Copy and edit secrets**
   ```bash
   cp secrets.yaml.example secrets.yaml
   # Fill in WiFi, static IP, encryption key, OTA password
   ```

2. **Flash the firmware** (USB-C, near your workstation, NOT yet in the mailbox)
   ```bash
   esphome run mailbox-notifier.yaml
   ```
   Test the wake cycle on the bench first. Touch the magnet to the reed switch and watch the logs.

3. **Wire it up** following the diagram above. Solder, do not breadboard. Vibration from the mailbox lid will loosen breadboard contacts within weeks.

4. **Apply conformal coating** to the ESP32 PCB and the TP4056 board. Avoid the antenna trace and the USB-C connector. Let it cure 24 hours before assembly.

5. **Pack the enclosure** with 2 to 4 grams of fresh silica gel. Seal cable entries with self-amalgamating tape, NOT silicone (silicone outgasses acetic acid which corrodes copper).

6. **Mount the reed switch** on the inside of the mailbox frame, near the hinge edge. Glue the magnet to the lid so that closing the lid brings the magnet next to the reed switch. Verify with a multimeter before screwing the lid shut.

7. **Mount the enclosure** inside or under the mailbox. If WiFi is weak, run the antenna pigtail out to an SMA-mounted antenna positioned higher up.

8. **Import the Home Assistant pieces**:
   - Add the `input_datetime.last_mail_arrival` helper (Settings -> Devices and Services -> Helpers -> Date and time)
   - Copy `automations.yaml` content into your automations
   - Add the `dashboard.yaml` view to your Lovelace dashboard

## WiFi reception at the mailbox

This is the single biggest reason mailbox notifiers fail. The mailbox is usually at the property line, often 10 to 30 meters from your router, often with a wall or a hedge in the way, and often inside a metal enclosure.

**Diagnose before you build:**
- Walk to the mailbox with your phone, check signal strength
- If your phone reports below -75 dBm, you need an external antenna
- If your phone cannot connect at all, you need either a mesh extender on the house wall or a different mailbox design (LoRa, for example, which is out of scope here)

**Mitigations, in order of effectiveness:**

1. **Plastic mailbox**: if you have a choice, plastic is far better than metal
2. **External antenna**: snap off the PCB antenna ceramic chip, solder a uFL connector, run a short pigtail to an external 2.4 GHz antenna (5 to 8 dBi) mounted outside the enclosure
3. **Antenna placement**: vertical orientation, away from the ESP32 board, away from the battery, ideally 10 to 20 cm of clear air around it
4. **Move the router**: a mesh node on the side of the house facing the mailbox often solves everything
5. **fast_connect + static IP**: already enabled in this config, shaves 1 to 2 seconds off each wake cycle (less time awake = more battery)

## Outdoor durability

Mailboxes are a brutal environment. Daily temperature swings drive condensation, and condensation kills bare electronics within months.

**Thermal range expected:**
- Sweden / Northern Europe: -25 C to +40 C
- Internal mailbox temperature can exceed ambient by 20 C in direct summer sun

**The 18650 choice matters:**
- LiPo pouch cells stop accepting charge below 0 C and degrade fast in summer heat
- 18650 LiPo cells (Samsung 30Q, Sony VTC6, Panasonic NCR18650B) tolerate -20 C to +60 C in storage and operate well from -10 C upward
- Discharge capacity drops about 30% at -20 C but recovers when warm. That is fine for this duty cycle.

**Condensation strategy:**
1. **Seal the enclosure** with the cable gland tight, lid gasket clean, screws snug but not over-tightened (over-tightening warps the lid and breaks the seal)
2. **Silica gel inside**: 2 to 4 grams of indicating silica gel. Check every 6 months. Color change from blue to pink means replace or bake.
3. **Conformal coating on the PCB**: acrylic brushable coating is cheap and adds a year of life
4. **Do not put the enclosure on the cold metal wall** of the mailbox. Use plastic standoffs or foam padding to give the case its own thermal mass and reduce dew point crossing events.

## Battery life expectations

| Scenario | Estimated life |
|----------|----------------|
| 2 wake events per day, good WiFi, static IP | 10 to 14 months |
| 5 wake events per day, weak WiFi (slow reconnect) | 5 to 7 months |
| Reed switch chattering (vibration without magnet leaving) | 1 to 2 weeks (fix the mounting!) |

If you see battery draining faster than expected, check the logs for repeated wake events. Reed switches near the edge of the magnetic field can bounce. The 50 ms debounce in `mailbox-notifier.yaml` helps but is not magic; mount the magnet so it clearly enters and leaves the field, do not park it at the boundary.

## Configuration notes

### Why GPIO33 for the reed switch

RTC GPIOs on ESP32-WROOM-32 that work for `ext0` deep sleep wake: 0, 2, 4, 12, 13, 14, 15, 25, 26, 27, 32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38, 39. Of these, GPIO33 has an internal pull-up, is not a strapping pin, and is not used by flash. It is the cleanest choice for a normally-open reed switch wired to GND.

### Why GPIO34 for battery sensing

GPIO34 is input-only (no internal pull-up or pull-down to leak current through), is on ADC1 (ADC1 works during WiFi, ADC2 does not), and is not a strapping pin.

### Why deep_sleep with no run_duration

This config does not set `run_duration` on the deep sleep component. Instead, a `script` enters deep sleep manually once the API has confirmed delivery. This pattern is more battery-friendly than a fixed timeout: a fast wake cycle (good WiFi, fast API) finishes in 4 to 6 seconds; a slow one (weak WiFi) waits up to 15 seconds for the API connection before giving up.

### Why fast_connect: true

Skips the WiFi scan phase, connecting directly to the last-known BSSID. Saves about 1 second per wake. Trade-off: if your router moves BSSIDs or you swap APs, the device will fail one wake, then fall back to a full scan on the next. Worth it.

## Troubleshooting

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Never wakes | Reed switch not on RTC pin, or pin pulled wrong direction | Verify GPIO33, verify pull-up, verify magnet polarity |
| Wakes constantly | Vibration or boundary mounting | Re-mount magnet so it clearly enters/leaves the field |
| Wakes but no notification | WiFi unreachable, API key wrong | Check signal in HA, check encryption key matches |
| Battery dies in weeks | Probably constant wakes, or LDO leaking | Check logs for wake frequency, measure sleep current (should be ~50 microamps total) |
| Drops offline in winter | LiPo too cold | Switch to 18650 LiPo if not already, or relocate enclosure |
