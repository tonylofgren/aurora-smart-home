# ESP32 Pool / Spa Controller

> *Generated by [aurora@aurora-smart-home (esphome skill)](https://github.com/tonylofgren/aurora-smart-home)*

A complete ESPHome + Home Assistant package for monitoring and automating a residential swimming pool or spa. The controller continuously measures water temperature, pH and ORP (oxidation-reduction potential, used as a chlorine proxy), drives the main circulation pump, and pulses two peristaltic dosing pumps for liquid chlorine and pH minus. It enforces daily dosing caps, refuses to dose without confirmed water flow, and alerts Home Assistant when chemistry drifts outside the safe range.

## What you get

- Live water temperature from a DS18B20 probe with 0.1 C resolution
- Calibratable pH and ORP readings from Atlas Scientific EZO circuits over I2C
- Safe, rate-limited dosing of chlorine and pH minus via peristaltic pumps
- Time-of-day pump schedule with Summer / Winter / Service modes
- Daily dosing counters with operator-settable caps
- Flow-switch interlock that blocks dosing whenever circulation stops
- Lovelace dashboard with glance card, trends and limits
- Alert automations for pH or ORP out of safe range for 30+ minutes

## Bill of materials

| Part | Notes |
| ---- | ----- |
| ESP32 dev board (30-pin) | Any module with 4 MB flash works |
| DS18B20 waterproof probe | Mount in a brass thermowell on the return line |
| 4.7 kOhm resistor | One-wire pull-up between data and 3V3 |
| Atlas Scientific EZO-pH circuit + lab-grade pH probe | I2C mode, default address 0x63 |
| Atlas Scientific EZO-ORP circuit + ORP probe | I2C mode, default address 0x62 |
| Carrier board for EZO circuits | Either the Atlas carrier or any breakout exposing SDA/SCL/Vcc/GND |
| 2-channel SSR or relay board (230 V AC) | For the main circulation pump |
| 2-channel low-side MOSFET or relay board (12 V) | For the two peristaltic dosing pumps |
| 2 x peristaltic dosing pumps (12 V, Viton or PharMed tubing) | Sized to 1.5 ml/s nominal |
| Paddle or Hall-effect flow switch | Plumbed on the return line, normally open |
| IP54+ enclosure with cable glands | Outdoor mounting |
| 12 V / 2 A and 5 V / 2 A PSUs | Or one 12 V PSU plus a buck converter |

Atlas Scientific EZO circuits are the standard hobbyist and small-commercial choice because the probes are isolated from logic ground (which is essential when the main pump is also bonded to the pool's grounding grid), and they are field-calibratable from ESPHome or from Home Assistant.

## Wiring

```
ESP32           Peripheral
-----           ----------
GPIO21  ----->  SDA (EZO pH + EZO ORP, daisy-chained)
GPIO22  ----->  SCL
GPIO4   ----->  DS18B20 data (with 4.7 kOhm to 3V3)
GPIO25  ----->  Main pump SSR control
GPIO26  ----->  Chlorine dosing pump relay
GPIO27  ----->  pH minus dosing pump relay
GPIO32  <-----  Flow switch (NO contact to GND, INPUT_PULLUP)
3V3     ----->  EZO Vcc, DS18B20 Vcc, flow switch pull-up
GND     ----->  Common ground
```

Power note: keep the 230 V AC side of the SSR completely separate from the 12 V dosing side, and ensure every probe well is bonded to the pool's equipotential grounding grid per local electrical code. If you are not licensed to wire pool equipment, hire someone who is. Mistakes here are not a software bug; they are a swimmer-safety bug.

## Quick start

1. Copy `secrets.yaml.example` to `secrets.yaml` and fill in real values. Generate the API encryption key with `openssl rand -base64 32`.
2. Run `esphome compile pool-controller.yaml`, then `esphome upload pool-controller.yaml --device /dev/ttyUSB0` for the first flash. After that, OTA over WiFi.
3. Adopt the device in Home Assistant via the ESPHome integration.
4. Drop `automations.yaml` into your `packages/` directory (or merge into `automations.yaml`).
5. Add the contents of `dashboard.yaml` as a new view in your Lovelace dashboard.
6. Calibrate the pH and ORP probes before relying on dosing (see Calibration below).

## Pool chemistry crash course

A well-balanced residential pool sits at:

- **pH 7.2 to 7.6.** Below 7.0 the water becomes acidic, etches plaster and corrodes metal fittings (heater elements first). Above 7.8 chlorine loses up to 80 percent of its disinfecting power and scale starts forming on heaters and salt cells. This controller targets 7.2 to 7.6 by default.
- **Free chlorine 1 to 3 ppm.** You do not measure this directly; instead the controller measures ORP, which is the millivolt potential at which all dissolved oxidisers (chlorine, bromine, ozone) collectively sit. For chlorinated pools at pH 7.4, an ORP of about 650 to 750 mV corresponds roughly to 1 to 3 ppm free chlorine. CDC guidance treats 650 mV as the minimum for safe disinfection.
- **Total alkalinity 80 to 120 ppm.** This is the pH buffer; you adjust it manually with sodium bicarbonate. The controller does not touch alkalinity, but a low or unstable pH usually means low alkalinity.
- **Cyanuric acid (stabiliser) 30 to 50 ppm outdoors, 0 ppm indoors.** Without it, UV destroys free chlorine in hours; with too much, chlorine becomes sluggish and ORP under-reads.
- **Calcium hardness 200 to 400 ppm.** Too low and the water dissolves plaster; too high and calcium scales out on heaters. Not measured here.

ORP is not the same as ppm. ORP is what actually kills pathogens (it is the redox potential available to oxidise cell walls), so it is the better closed-loop control variable. But the ORP-to-ppm relationship depends strongly on pH, cyanuric acid and temperature, so always verify the ORP target against a DPD or FAS-DPD test kit when first commissioning the system, and every spring.

### Why dose pH minus and chlorine separately

Liquid chlorine (sodium hypochlorite, ~12.5 percent) is strongly alkaline. Every time you chlorinate, you push pH upward, which then reduces chlorine effectiveness, which prompts the controller to dose more chlorine, which pushes pH higher again. This positive-feedback loop is why every automated pool also doses an acid. The controller handles the two loops independently:

1. **pH loop**: if pH stays above the configurable high target for 5 minutes (default 7.6), dose 5 ml of pH minus, then wait 30 minutes for the water to circulate and re-measure.
2. **ORP loop**: if ORP stays below the low target for 5 minutes (default 650 mV), dose 10 ml of liquid chlorine, then wait 30 minutes.

The 30-minute settling delay is critical. Without it, the controller would over-shoot massively because peristaltic doses take 5 to 15 minutes to mix through the pool volume.

## Safety design

This controller is operating two pumps that can pour concentrated acid and bleach into a body of water that humans swim in. Several layers of protection are built in:

1. **Flow interlock.** No dose can fire unless the flow switch on the return line reads "flow detected" and the main pump is on. A dosing pump running into a dead line will eventually inject neat chemical into one spot when flow resumes, creating a chlorine cloud or an acid pocket that will burn skin.
2. **Daily caps.** Each chemical has an operator-settable daily cap (default 200 ml chlorine, 100 ml pH minus). Once the running total exceeds the cap, dosing is blocked until midnight resets the counter. If you are hitting the cap repeatedly, something else is wrong (algae bloom, broken probe, leak, swimmer load way above design) and a human needs to look.
3. **Service mode.** Setting the operating mode to "Service" suspends all dosing and pump schedules so you can backwash, replace cartridges, or shock the pool manually without the controller fighting you.
4. **Out-of-range alerts.** If pH stays above 8.0 or below 6.8 for 30 minutes, or ORP stays below 550 mV or above 850 mV for 30 minutes, Home Assistant sends a push notification. These thresholds are tighter than the dosing thresholds because the controller should normally hold the water inside them; breaching them means dosing is not keeping up.
5. **Restore mode on dosing pumps.** Dosing-pump relays use `restore_mode: ALWAYS_OFF`, so any reboot, brown-out, or firmware flash leaves the chemical pumps physically off. The main pump uses `RESTORE_DEFAULT_OFF` so it can come back on under HA control.
6. **Sliding-window average on temperature.** Three-sample moving average suppresses single-sample DS18B20 glitches that would otherwise look like a pool freeze and trigger the winter circulation automation needlessly.

## Calibration

Atlas Scientific probes drift slowly with age and faster with fouling. Plan to calibrate:

- **pH probe**: every 3 months, or whenever readings disagree with a fresh test strip by more than 0.2. Use Atlas's pH 4.00, 7.00 and 10.00 calibration solutions. Rinse with distilled water between buffers.
- **ORP probe**: every 6 months, or whenever readings disagree with a 225 mV calibration solution by more than 25 mV.

This config exposes two services for calibration:

```yaml
# Three-point pH calibration
action: esphome.pool_controller_calibrate_ph
data:
  point: "mid"   # then "low", then "high" - dunk probe in each buffer first
```

```yaml
# Single-point ORP calibration against the Atlas 225 mV solution
action: esphome.pool_controller_calibrate_orp
data:
  mv: 225
```

Wait at least 60 seconds in each buffer with gentle stirring before issuing the calibration call so the EZO circuit's running average has settled.

## Operating modes

- **Summer.** Pump runs 09:00 to 17:00 (8 hours). pH and ORP loops are armed. This is the only mode that doses.
- **Winter.** Pump is off by default. If water temperature drops below 4 C the pump runs for 10 minutes to prevent freeze damage in the plumbing. Dosing remains suspended; in cold water chlorine demand drops to near zero and pH drifts very slowly, so any drift can be corrected manually in spring.
- **Service.** All automation halted. Use this when shocking the pool, backwashing the filter, or replacing chemical drums.

You can edit the schedule by changing the `time` triggers in `automations.yaml`. For larger pools, 12 hours of pump time (06:00 to 18:00) is more typical; for spas with a much smaller volume but a heavier bather load relative to volume, 4 to 6 hours is enough. The right number is whatever circulates the full pool volume at least once per day, ideally twice.

## Sizing the dose

Default dose volumes (5 ml pH minus, 10 ml chlorine per cycle) are sized for a 40 m3 (~10 500 US gallon) pool with the recommended 1.5 ml/s peristaltic pumps. For a different pool volume, scale linearly:

```
dose_ml_new = dose_ml_default * (pool_volume_m3 / 40)
```

A 20 m3 spa thus needs roughly 2.5 ml pH minus and 5 ml chlorine per cycle. Adjust the values in the two dosing automations in `automations.yaml`.

If you change to faster or slower peristaltic pumps, update `number.peristaltic_pump_flow_rate` from the dashboard. The controller divides the requested dose volume by this flow rate to compute the relay pulse width, so getting it right within +/- 10 percent is what keeps dose volumes accurate.

## Troubleshooting

- **pH reads exactly 7.00 forever**: the EZO is in factory state and has never been calibrated, or the probe is disconnected. Check I2C with `i2cdetect` from a Pi or via ESPHome's `scan: true` log line.
- **ORP reads negative or pinned at 0**: the platinum tip is fouled. Clean with the Atlas 0.1 M HCl restore solution, then recalibrate.
- **Doses fire but chemistry does not change**: the tube to the injection point is air-locked, or the check valve at the injection point has failed. Prime the tubing by running the dosing pump manually for 30 seconds with the suction tube in the chemical drum.
- **Flow switch never reads "on"**: paddle switches need flow above their minimum trip point (typically 4 to 8 L/min). If your pump is variable-speed and runs slow most of the time, raise the lower speed or use a Hall-effect flow sensor instead.
- **Daily cap reached every day**: verify probe calibration first. If chemistry is genuinely demanding more chemical, raise the cap or investigate the cause (high bather load, sun load, algae). Do not blindly raise caps without testing the water.
