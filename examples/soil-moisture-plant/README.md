# Soil Moisture Plant Monitor

> *Generated by [aurora@aurora-smart-home (esphome skill)](https://github.com/tonylofgren/aurora-smart-home)*

Battery-powered plant care monitor that watches soil moisture, air
temperature, and air humidity, then pings Home Assistant when the plant
gets thirsty. Runs for around six months on a single 18650 LiPo cell.

## Features

- **Capacitive soil moisture probe** with user calibration (per soil type)
- **Air temperature and humidity** via the precision SHT40 sensor
- **Deep sleep** between readings: 30 minute interval by default
- **Battery voltage monitoring** with low-battery notification
- **Thirsty plant alerts** when soil drops below your threshold
- **Optional auto-water** via a smart plug controlling a small pump
- **OTA updates** during the brief awake window

## Why capacitive (and not resistive)

The cheap resistive probes sold as "soil moisture sensors" are the wrong
tool for the job. They work by passing DC current between two exposed
metal traces in the soil. Two things happen:

1. **Electrolysis.** DC through wet soil ionises the moisture and eats
   the electrode metal. The exposed traces oxidise and physically rust
   through.
2. **Drift.** As the surface corrodes, contact resistance changes, so the
   readings drift even before the probe dies.

In my own testing a resistive probe in a potted plant rusted through in
about a month. Capacitive probes (the blue v1.2 board from AliExpress
that this example is built around) use a coated capacitor as the sensing
element. No exposed metal, no DC current through the soil, no corrosion.
They typically last for years.

If you only remember one thing from this README: **buy the capacitive
one**. The price difference is about one euro.

## Hardware list

### Controller

- Seeed Xiao ESP32-C3 (USB-C, tiny, low quiescent current in deep sleep)

### Sensors and power

| Component | Purpose | Interface |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Capacitive soil moisture sensor v1.2 | Soil water content | ADC |
| Sensirion SHT40 | Air temperature and humidity | I2C |
| 18650 LiPo cell + holder | Power | DC |
| TP4056 charger module (with protection IC) | Charge management | USB-C in |
| 2x 100k ohm resistors | Battery voltage divider | Wired |

### Bill of materials cost

Around 12 to 18 EUR total from common European retailers. The Xiao and
SHT40 are the most expensive items.

## Wiring

```
Xiao ESP32-C3                Soil sensor (capacitive v1.2)
   3V3  ----------------->   VCC
   GND  ----------------->   GND
   GPIO2 (A0) ----------->   AOUT

Xiao ESP32-C3                SHT40 breakout
   3V3  ----------------->   VCC
   GND  ----------------->   GND
   GPIO6 (SDA) ---------->   SDA
   GPIO7 (SCL) ---------->   SCL

Battery monitoring (voltage divider)
   BAT+ ---[100k]---+---[100k]--- GND
                    |
                    +--- GPIO3 (A1)

Power
   TP4056 OUT+ -----> Xiao BAT pin (or 5V if your TP4056 boosts)
   TP4056 OUT- -----> Xiao GND
   18650 cell -----> TP4056 B+ / B-
```

Do not skip the voltage divider. The Xiao ADC tops out around 3.3 V, but
a full LiPo sits at 4.2 V. The divider halves it, and the YAML multiplies
the reading back up by two.

## Installation

1. **Copy the secrets template**:
   ```bash
   cp secrets.yaml.example secrets.yaml
   ```
   Fill in `wifi_ssid`, `wifi_password`, `api_encryption_key`, and
   `ota_password`.

2. **Flash the Xiao**:
   ```bash
   esphome run soil-moisture-plant.yaml
   ```

3. **Mount the probe**:
   - Push the capacitive probe into the soil up to (but not past) the
     white line printed on the PCB. The line marks the maximum safe
     immersion depth.
   - Keep the electronics end at least a few centimetres above the soil
     surface so condensation does not pool on the connector.

4. **Calibrate** (see next section). Default calibration values work
   reasonably well for general potting mix, but each soil type holds
   water differently.

5. **Add the automations** from `automations.yaml` to Home Assistant.

6. **Import the dashboard** from `dashboard.yaml`.

## Calibration

The capacitive probe outputs a voltage between roughly 1.1 V (in water)
and 2.7 V (in dry air). The exact endpoints depend on the supply voltage
and the individual probe, so you should measure yours.

1. **Disable deep sleep**: turn on `switch.plant_monitor_prevent_sleep`
   in Home Assistant. The node will stay awake so you can read it live.

2. **Dry reading**: hold the probe in dry air, not touching anything.
   Wait 10 seconds for the median filter to settle. Note
   `sensor.plant_monitor_soil_voltage`. This is your `voltage_dry`.

3. **Wet reading**: dip the probe in a glass of water, up to but not
   past the white line. Wait 10 seconds. Note the voltage. This is your
   `voltage_wet`.

4. **Update the YAML substitutions** at the top of
   `soil-moisture-plant.yaml`:
   ```yaml
   substitutions:
     voltage_dry: "2.65"
     voltage_wet: "1.15"
   ```

5. **Reflash** with `esphome run soil-moisture-plant.yaml`.

6. **Turn deep sleep back on** by switching off `Prevent Sleep`.

You can recalibrate for each pot if you want to be precise, but in
practice one calibration covers most common potting mixes.

## Battery life

A 2000 mAh 18650 cell at the default 30 minute interval lasts about
six months. The numbers:

| State | Current draw | Time per cycle |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| Deep sleep | ~50 uA | 30 min |
| WiFi connect + sample + publish | ~80 mA | ~3 s |

That works out to roughly 0.07 mA average draw. 2000 mAh / 0.07 mA is
around 28,000 hours, but real life adds router reconnects, occasional
OTA polling, and self-discharge. Six months is a safe planning number.

If you want longer:

- **Lengthen `sleep_duration`.** Doubling to 60 minutes nearly doubles
  the runtime. Plants change slowly, this is usually fine.
- **Shorten `awake_duration`.** The default 10 seconds leaves room for
  OTA. If your WiFi is fast, 6 seconds is plenty.
- **Use a larger cell.** A 3500 mAh 21700 cell with the same TP4056
  setup gets you most of a year.

## Automations included

- **Thirsty notification** when soil stays below threshold for 10 minutes
- **Auto-water** via a smart plug controlling a small water pump
- **Low battery warning** at 20%

The auto-water automation expects a `switch.plant_pump` entity. Wire a
small 5V or 12V pump to a smart plug, name the entity to match, and the
pump runs for an 8 second burst when soil drops below 25 percent. A
60 minute cool-down prevents repeated bursts before the soil reading
catches up.

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Solution |
|-------|----------|
| Moisture reads 0 or 100 constantly | Recalibrate. The dry / wet voltages depend on your specific probe. |
| Readings jitter wildly | Make sure the probe is fully inserted to the white line. Air gaps cause noise. |
| Node never reconnects after a router reboot | `reboot_timeout: 5min` in the YAML handles this. If it persists, check WiFi signal strength. |
| Battery dies in days | Most likely the node is not entering deep sleep. Check that `prevent_sleep` is OFF and look for OTA mode getting stuck. |
| SHT40 not found | Check I2C wiring. The default address is 0x44. Run `esphome logs` and look for the i2c scan. |
| Auto-water never triggers | Check `switch.plant_pump` exists and the soil reading actually drops below 25%. The 15 minute trigger duration prevents brief dips from firing it. |

## Safety notes

- The TP4056 module must be the version **with battery protection**
  (the small one with two ICs on the underside). Without protection, an
  18650 can over-discharge and become a fire hazard.
- Keep the soil probe electronics above the soil line. The probe is
  coated, but the connector and the trimmer pot are not.
- Do not water the dashboard.
